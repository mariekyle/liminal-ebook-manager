# Liminal — Voice & Tone Guidelines

How Liminal speaks to its users: warm, calm, unhurried, and genuinely welcoming.

**Relationship to other docs:** this document is the law. `MICROCOPY_LIBRARY.md` holds the approved strings that follow it. When the two disagree, this document wins and the library gets corrected.

---

## Before the Words: Purpose & Anticipation

Two questions come before any copy is written:

1. **What is this screen's one job?** Every screen has one. If the copy is hard to write, the job is unclear — fix that first.
2. **What will the reader wonder next?** Every message answers the user's likely next question, ideally before they ask it. Changing a status to DNF quietly answers the unspoken "is that okay?" with "No need to finish everything."

---

## The Liminal Voice

If Liminal were a person, it would be:

- **A librarian who loves their collection** — knowledgeable but never condescending
- **A friend who reads** — shares enthusiasm without pressure
- **A cozy bookshop** — inviting, warm, no rush to buy
- **A reading nook** — quiet, personal, yours

Liminal is NOT:

- A productivity coach tracking your goals
- A social platform comparing you to others
- A store trying to sell you something
- A teacher grading your progress

---

## Tone Spectrum

Liminal's tone lives in a specific range:

```
Clinical ←───────────────────────────────────→ Precious
         |                             |
         |     ✓ LIMINAL LIVES HERE    |
         |                             |
      Too cold                      Too soft
      Too formal                    Too whimsical
      Data-focused                  Loses meaning
```

### Too Clinical (avoid)
> "You have 847 unread items. Last activity: 6 months ago."

### Too Precious (avoid)
> "Oh, sweet reader! Your magical book babies miss you! 🌸✨"

### Just Right (Liminal)
> "847 reads waiting to be remembered."

## Sentence Rhythm

### Short phrases for ambient moments
Microcopy should feel like a whisper, not a paragraph.

**Good:**
- "Wander freely."
- "No rush."
- "Pick it up anytime."

**Too long:**
- "Feel free to take your time browsing through your collection at whatever pace feels comfortable to you."

### When longer is appropriate
Descriptions, help text, and onboarding can use fuller sentences. But even then, keep it conversational.

**Good:**
> "This is where your notes live. Add thoughts whenever you're ready — no pressure."

**Too formal:**
> "The notes section allows users to input and store personal annotations related to the selected title."

---

## Formality Level

Liminal is **warm casual** — like a knowledgeable friend, not a corporate app or a children's toy.

| Too Formal | Too Casual | Liminal |
|------------|------------|---------|
| "Select a category to filter results" | "Tap stuff to find stuff!" | "Filter by category" |
| "No results were returned for your query" | "Oopsie, nothing here!" | "Nothing found — try different words?" |
| "Item successfully added to library" | "Yay, you did it!" | "Added to your library" |
| "Confirm deletion of this item?" | "Bye bye book?" | "Remove from library?" |

---

## Punctuation Guidelines

### Ambient phrases: No period
Short poetic phrases feel lighter without terminal punctuation.

- ✓ "Wander freely"
- ✓ "No rush"
- ✓ "A quick visit"
- ✗ "Wander freely."

### Complete sentences: Period okay
When writing full sentences, punctuation is natural.

- ✓ "Nothing here expires."
- ✓ "The right one will find you."

### Questions: Use sparingly
Questions can feel demanding. Use them gently.

- ✓ "Which one calls to you?"
- ✓ "Browse and discover?"
- ✗ "What do you want to read??"

### Exclamation marks: Almost never
Exclamation marks add urgency and energy — the opposite of calm.

- ✗ "Welcome back!"
- ✗ "Great choice!"
- ✓ "Welcome back"
- ✓ "A great find"

**Exception:** Moments of genuine celebration (completing a long series, reaching a milestone) might warrant gentle enthusiasm. Even then, one is enough.

## Tone by Moment

The voice is constant; the tone varies with the moment. The further an action's consequences reach, the plainer the language gets.

| Moment | Register | Example |
|--------|----------|---------|
| Ambient (library phrases, time descriptors, soft milestones, first-run) | Poetic welcome | "1,687 doors. Wander freely." |
| Routine action (navigation, buttons, filters, search, settings) | Plain and functional | "Library" / "Filter by category" |
| Transient feedback (toasts) | Plain, past tense, brief | "Added to your library" |
| Celebration (finishing a saga, a soft milestone) | One notch warmer; a single "!" at most | "A true saga, finished" |
| Error | Plain, calm, specific | "Couldn't save your changes. Try again?" |
| Destructive confirmation | Plainest register; zero poetry | "Remove from library?" → [Remove] [Keep] |

### Where ambient copy lives

| Location | Content type | Example |
|----------|--------------|---------|
| Library view, below filters | Count + phrase | "1,687 doors. Wander freely." |
| Book detail — reading time | Time + descriptor | "About 6 hours / a deeper dive" |
| Book detail — status | Soft progress | "Waiting on your shelf" |
| Stats page | Celebrated milestones | "You've visited 47 worlds this year" |
| Filter selected | Contextual phrase | "12 epics. When you're ready to commit." |
| Empty states | Warmth clause only (see Empty States) | "…No rush." |

### Where copy stays practical

| Location | Keep it simple |
|----------|----------------|
| Navigation tabs | "Library" / "Series" / "Stats" |
| Button labels | Verbs that name the action (see Buttons & Confirmations) |
| Search placeholder | "Search your library..." |
| Dropdown options | Clear, scannable labels |
| Settings | Functional descriptions |
| Error messages | Clear problem + solution |
| Confirmations | Question + verb answers |
| Screen reader labels | Functional register, always |

**Example — Navigation:**
- ✓ "Library" / "Series" / "Stats" (clear, functional)
- ✗ "Your Worlds" / "Sagas" / "Quiet Hours" (too clever, hinders usability)

**Example — The same screen, different contexts:**

Navigation tab: **"Library"** (practical)

Ambient phrase below: **"1,687 doors. Wander freely."** (poetic)

The practical scaffolding lets the poetic moments shine.

## Word Choice Principles

### Prefer active invitation over passive description

| Passive | Active Invitation |
|---------|-------------------|
| "Books can be browsed by category" | "Browse by category" |
| "Filters are available" | "Filter by mood, length, or category" |
| "Notes may be added" | "Add notes whenever you're ready" |

### Prefer warmth over efficiency

| Efficient | Warm |
|-----------|------|
| "0 results" | "Nothing found" |
| "Not started" | "Waiting on your shelf" |
| "Abandoned" | "DNF" |
| "8h 24m" | "About 8 hours" |

### Prefer possibility over judgment

| Judgmental | Possibility |
|------------|-------------|
| "You abandoned this" | "Pick it up anytime" |
| "847 unread books" | "847 reads waiting" |
| "Failed to finish" | "DNF" |
| "Disliked" | "Not for me" |

## Inclusive Language

### The library contains multitudes
Not everything in the library is a novel. Not everything is a "story." Scientific papers, guidebooks, analyses, fanfiction, and fiction all live here — the language should never make any of them feel like second-class citizens.

### Term hierarchy

**Universal terms** — safe for any content type, use these by default:

- **Reads** — casual, warm, works for everything
- **Works** — slightly more formal, borrowed from AO3, fits all formats
- **Titles** — functional, neutral, good for UI labels

**Poetic/metaphorical terms** — safe at the collection level (describing the library as a whole) but not for individual items:

- Doors, worlds, journeys, thresholds, paths

**Genre-appropriate terms** — use only in genre-filtered contexts:

- **Stories** — Fiction and FanFiction only
- **Papers, guides** — Non-Fiction only (if ever needed)

**Avoid:**

- **"Books"** — many items were never physical books
- **"Stories"** as a universal fallback — a research paper isn't a story

### When to use poetic vs. neutral language for items

Poetic metaphors (doors, worlds, journeys) work well when describing the **collection as a whole** — they set atmosphere without making claims about individual items.

They break down when applied to **individual items**, especially nonfiction. "The door is still open" works for a half-read novel. It's strange for a half-read paper on molecular biology.

| Context | Approach | Example |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Library-level ambient phrase | Poetic metaphors welcome | "1,687 doors. Wander freely." |
| Individual fiction/fanfic item | Poetic okay | "A third of the way through" |
| Individual nonfiction item | Keep it neutral | "A third of the way through" (still fine — it's genre-neutral) |
| Status labels (any genre) | Always clear and functional | "DNF" / "Finished" / "In progress" |

**Rule of thumb:** If it would sound odd applied to a technical manual, use a neutral alternative.

### Carve-out: system-defined descriptor labels

Fixed labels the app itself defines — milestone names ("Worlds Visited," "Quiet Hours"), reading-time tiers ("a deeper dive," "a true saga"), filter bucket names — may stay poetic even when the underlying items are mixed-genre. **They name the measure or the shelf, not the item.** "Worlds Visited" is a stat's name the same way "library" contains things that were never books; a molecular-biology paper counting toward it doesn't break the metaphor.

The technical-manual test applies to language written *about an individual work* ("The door is still open"), not to the app's own fixtures. This is also why "Quiet Hours" is fine as a stat name but wrong as a navigation tab: fixtures may be poetic, wayfinding may not.

### Fanfiction deserves respect
Fanfiction is a labor of love, created freely by passionate fans. The language should never diminish it.

- Don't call them "just fanfics"
- "Works" is respectful (borrowed from AO3)
- "Gifts from fellow dreamers" honors the community

### Reading happens on many schedules
Avoid assuming when people read:

- ✗ "An evening's read"
- ✗ "A lazy afternoon"
- ✓ "A few hours"
- ✓ "Room to settle in"

## Status Language

Status labels must be **unambiguous above all else.** A label you have to decode every time you see it has failed, no matter how gentle it sounds.

### Status Labels

| Status | Label | Notes |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Not started | "Waiting on your shelf" | Warm, but unmistakable |
| In progress | "In progress" | Can pair with qualitative progress (see below) |
| Finished | "Finished" | Clean, satisfying |
| Did not finish | "DNF" | The reading community already uses this without shame. Clear, honest, zero ambiguity. |

**Implementation note:** the internal database value is `abandoned`; the display label comes from `useStatusLabels`, is user-configurable, and defaults to "DNF." "Abandoned" never renders in the UI.

### Why "DNF" and not a softer alternative

"DNF" wins because:
- **Clarity** — everyone who reads knows what it means
- **No shame** — the reading community has already normalized it
- **No ambiguity** — unlike "set aside" or "paused," it's unambiguous about intent
- **Brevity** — fits neatly in tags, filters, and status chips

### Surrounding language can still be warm

The label is functional. The *context* around it can still be gentle:

- Filter showing DNF'd items: "[count] reads you moved on from"
- Empty DNF shelf: "Nothing here yet. Reads you set aside will show up here."
- Changing status to DNF: "No need to finish everything"

### Progress Display Options

**Soft version (qualitative):**
- "Just begun"
- "A third of the way through"
- "About halfway"
- "More than halfway"
- "Almost there"
- "Finished"

**Hybrid version (number + context):**
- "12% — just getting started"
- "34% — a third of the way through"
- "52% — past the halfway mark"
- "89% — almost there"

### Long-Unread Items

For items started but not touched in a while:

- Not: "Last read: 6 months ago"
- Instead: "Paused for a while. Ready when you are."

Or for fiction/fanfic specifically: "You began this one 9 months ago. Pick it up anytime."

## Buttons & Confirmations

Buttons name their action. Never Yes / No / OK.

**The button-only test:** if someone reads nothing but the buttons, they should still know exactly what happens. `[Remove] [Keep]` passes. `[Yes] [Cancel]` fails.

### The confirmation pattern

The title asks the question; the buttons answer it.

> **Remove from library?**
> [Remove]  [Keep]

- **Destructive label = the specific verb.** "Remove," "Delete notes" — never a generic "Confirm."
- **Safe label = the safe outcome.** "Keep," "Keep notes" — never "Cancel." ("Cancel" gets genuinely dangerous anywhere cancelling *is* the action.)
- **Body text is optional.** Include it only to state a real consequence — "This also deletes your notes for it" — and only if the consequence is true. No padding, no ceremony.
- Liminal uses inline confirmations, never `window.confirm()`. The pattern is identical either way: question, then verb-labeled answers.

### Interaction tiers — which moments get which copy

| Tier | Interactions | Copy |
|------|--------------|------|
| Instant (no message) | Filters, view toggles, browsing, expanding sections | None — the UI change is the feedback |
| Subtle confirm (toast) | Rating, status change, saving notes, sync | Past tense, five words or fewer, no exclamation: "Added to your library" |
| Confirmation (inline dialog) | Removing a read, clearing notes, bulk actions | Question title + verb buttons |

### Recovery framing

When an action is reversible, say so — "Removed — Undo" beats a warning beforehand. Where undo isn't possible, the confirmation carries the weight instead. After-the-fact reassurance is welcome: "You can add it back anytime."

## Error Messages

Every error answers three things, in order:

1. **What happened** — specific and plain. Name the thing that failed. No codes.
2. **What to do next** — one clear step.
3. **A way to do it** — the primary action fixes the problem; a secondary action ("Not Now") lets them carry on.

> "Couldn't save your changes. Try again?"
> "Couldn't reach your library. Check your connection and try again."

### Rules

- **Never "Oops," "Uh-oh," or other interjections.** They read as patronizing.
- **Ration "sorry."** Only when something genuinely disruptive happened — data at risk, work interrupted. Never for routine hiccups; overuse makes it insincere.
- **Error codes never appear in the message body.** If one must exist for debugging, tuck it behind a details disclosure.
- **Reassure when data is safe** — "Your file is still on your device" — but only when it's true. Calm is built on honesty about consequences.
- **No silent failures.** Every failure the app knows about gets words. (The engineering rule lives in CLAUDE.md; this is its copy side.)

## Empty States

Formula: **what lives here + how it gets here** — then, optionally, one clause of warmth. Warmth is seasoning, not the meal.

| | |
|---|---|
| ✗ Warmth only | "Nothing here. Every read landed — or you haven't started enough yet" (no guidance, faint guilt trip) |
| ✓ Formula | "Nothing here yet. Reads you set aside will show up here." |
| ✓ Formula + warmth | "Nothing finished yet. Reads you complete will gather here. No rush." |

The approved set lives in `MICROCOPY_LIBRARY.md`.

## Screen Reader Text

Assistive text is copy, and it is always in the **functional register**. WCAG 2.1 AA is the standing commitment; these rules are the voice side of it.

- **Icon-only buttons get verb labels.** "Add to library," "Open filters," "Close." An unlabeled icon button is a silent failure.
- **Navigation announces plain names.** Tabs read "Library," "Series," "Stats" — never emoji descriptions.
- **Covers announce the work.** "Cover: [Title] by [Author]" — for extracted art and generated gradients alike. Never "gradient" or "placeholder."
- **Poetic text is visual-layer atmosphere.** Ambient phrases are marked decorative; the data they carry is exposed plainly ("1,687 titles").
- **No questions, no invitations.** "Filter by category," not "Which one calls to you?"

## Writing Checklist

Before finalizing any copy, ask:

- [ ] Would this feel at home in a calm, quiet space — warm without being cutesy, clear without being cold?
- [ ] Does it invite rather than demand?
- [ ] Does it acknowledge rather than guilt?
- [ ] Does this language work for a research paper as well as a novel?
- [ ] Am I using a universal term ("reads," "works," "titles") — or defaulting to a genre-specific one ("stories")?
- [ ] Am I assuming when or how people read?
- [ ] Is this the minimum words needed to communicate warmly?
- [ ] If this is a status label, is the meaning instantly obvious — or will I forget what it means in two weeks?
- [ ] **The skim test:** reading only the header and the buttons, does the screen still make sense?
- [ ] **The read-aloud test:** said out loud, does it sound like a person?
